\begin{abstract}
\noindent\textbf{Background.} Linkage mapping in outcrossing and open-pollinated
species is complicated by unknown paternity: offspring in a half-sib family share a
known mother but are sired by one or more unknown fathers, so the paternal contribution
to each offspring genotype cannot be observed directly \citep{GrattapagliaSederoff1994}.
\noindent\textbf{Results.} We present \textsf{HSMap}, an \textsf{R} package that infers
maternal recombination and linkage phase from open-pollinated diploid half-sib families
using a fast \textsf{C++} maternal hidden Markov model. The unknown paternal
contribution is integrated out of the emissions through a per-marker, dam-specific
paternal gametic frequency; multiple dams are combined by a joint
expectation--maximization (EM) algorithm that estimates a single shared recombination
map while keeping linkage phase and the paternal model dam-specific. Phase is inferred
externally from pairwise evidence and held fixed; the multipoint map is fitted only
within resolved phase blocks, and intervals with no linkage or unresolved phase are
reported as gaps rather than as inflated map distances.

\noindent\textbf{Conclusions.} \textsf{HSMap} provides a statistically explicit,
reproducible workflow for maternal linkage mapping when sires are unknown, with honest
reporting of unresolved phase and unlinked intervals.

\section{Introduction}
Linkage maps order molecular markers along chromosomes and quantify the recombination
between them, and they remain foundational for quantitative trait locus (QTL) mapping,
genome assembly validation, and the study of recombination itself
\citep{LanderBotstein1989}.
In many outcrossing plants and in open-pollinated breeding populations, the natural unit
of observation is a \emph{half-sib family}: a single known mother (dam) with many
offspring whose fathers are unknown and potentially numerous (a pollen pool), a design
central to linkage mapping in outcrossing species \citep{GrattapagliaSederoff1994}.
This design is attractive because such families are inexpensive to generate at scale,
but it poses a specific statistical difficulty for mapping: because the sire(s) are
unknown, the paternal allele transmitted to each offspring is a latent quantity, and the
usual full-sib machinery---which conditions on two known, phased parents---does not
apply.

A common response is to discard the paternal contribution by analysing only markers at
which the dam is heterozygous and treating the paternal allele as noise. This wastes
information and does not provide a principled likelihood. A more explicit alternative is
to model the transmission of the maternal homologs directly with a hidden Markov model
(HMM), as in multilocus linkage analysis \citep{LanderGreen1987}, and to integrate out
the unknown paternal allele at each marker.
Two features of the open-pollinated setting then demand care. First, the paternal
contribution is not a single sire's genotype but a \emph{pollen-pool} composition that
can differ between dams; it must be estimated, and it should be allowed to be
dam-specific. Second, because a single dam is heterozygous at only a subset of markers,
the linkage phase along a chromosome can be only partially identified from that dam's
data, and na\"ively forcing a phase where the data do not support one biases the map.

We present \textsf{HSMap}, an \textsf{R} package (with a \textsf{C++}/\textsf{Rcpp}
core) that addresses both issues. \textsf{HSMap} models maternal transmission with a
maternal-only HMM whose emissions marginalize the unknown paternal allele through a
per-marker, dam-specific \emph{paternal gametic frequency}; it estimates one shared
female recombination map across dams by a joint EM while keeping phase and the paternal
model dam-specific; it infers phase from pairwise evidence and, crucially, reports
intervals whose phase is unresolved rather than imputing them; and it fits the
multipoint map within resolved phase blocks, reporting unlinked intervals as gaps. The
remainder of the paper specifies the model and its estimation, describes the software,
and lays out the simulation and real-data studies that evaluate it. Support for
\emph{known}-sire full-sib crosses is under development and is discussed only as future
work.

\section{Software and methodological overview}
\textsf{HSMap} implements a single, reproducible workflow. Pedigree and genotype tables
are read into a family-structured data object; a two-point (pairwise) analysis estimates
a recombination fraction and a phase log-odds (LOD) score for every marker pair, sharing
the recombination fraction across informative families while estimating a dam-specific
paternal frequency; markers are clustered into linkage groups and ordered within each
group; each dam's linkage phase is assembled from the pairwise phase calls by a
signed-graph optimization that returns explicit resolved/unresolved metadata; the
multipoint maternal HMM is then fitted by EM---for a single dam, jointly across dams
sharing one map, or blockwise across regions of unresolved phase; and maps are summarized
with gap-aware centimorgan positions and plotted by phase block and map segment. The
statistical core is a two-state maternal HMM (Section~\ref{sec:transition}) whose
emissions integrate out the paternal allele through the dam-specific gametic frequency
(Section~\ref{emission}); parameters are estimated by forward--backward EM
(Section~\ref{sec:param_estimation}); phase is assembled externally
(Section~\ref{sec:graph_phase}); and blockwise fitting with gap-aware reporting handles
unresolved phase and no-linkage intervals (Section~\ref{sec:blocks}). The package,
documentation, and the small simulated example used throughout are openly available
(Section~\ref{sec:availability}). Figure~\ref{fig:method} summarizes the workflow and the
underlying model.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Overview}
We infer maternal recombination and linkage phase in diploid half-sib families with multiple dams and unknown sires. For each dam, transmission along an ordered marker set is modeled with a maternal-only hidden Markov model. The hidden chain identifies which maternal homolog is transmitted, transitions reflect recombination between adjacent markers, and emissions link the hidden homolog to observed offspring genotypes while averaging over the unknown paternal contribution.

We model inheritance along ordered markers with a hidden Markov model (HMM), where the latent state at marker $t$ encodes which maternal homolog was transmitted to each offspring. Transitions between states are governed by the recombination fractions between adjacent markers, and emissions are the offspring genotype likelihoods given the transmitted maternal homolog and a single scalar genotyping error rate (not a per-dam or per-marker error model). Parameters are estimated by maximum likelihood using forward and backward recursions with per-position scaling \citep{Rabiner1989}. To avoid introducing an additional latent layer for phase, we infer each dam’s chromosomal phase externally and hold it fixed during HMM fitting. When several dams are analyzed together, we fit one recombination map across families, while phase and emissions remain dam specific. Pooling families in a single framework increases the effective sample size for estimating recombination and is expected to improve precision when individual families are small; this expected benefit is evaluated in the simulation study (Section~\ref{sec:sim}).


We work under the following minimal assumptions: ($i$) the marker order is fixed; ($ii$) meioses are independent across offspring; ($iii$) a single female recombination map is shared across dams; ($iv$) genotyping errors are independent across loci and individuals; ($v$) allele transmission follows Mendelian segregation without distortion; ($vi$) for each dam $d$, the paternal contribution enters the offspring likelihood only through a single per-marker, dam-specific \emph{paternal gametic frequency} $q_k^{(d)}=\Pr(\text{a paternal gamete contributing to an offspring of dam }d\text{ transmits allele }A)$; in an open-pollinated family this is generally a dam-specific pollen-pool probability that may average over several unknown sires, rather than the allele frequency of a single sire, and each $q_k^{(d)}$ is a parameter estimated in the EM (detailed later); and ($vii$) at each marker the paternal gamete allele is marginalized conditional on $q_k^{(d)}$, \emph{independently across markers}, i.e.\ paternal linkage disequilibrium across markers is not modeled.

\subsection{Model Setup and Notation}
Let $M_1,\ldots,M_z$ denote $z$ ordered markers along a chromosome. The key parameter vector is $\mathbf r=(r_1,\ldots,r_{z-1})$, where $r_k\in[0,0.5]$ is the recombination fraction between markers $M_k$ and $M_{k+1}$. Let $n$ denote the total number of offspring considered across all dams.

We first define the model for a single dam and its offspring, then describe its extension to multiple families. A dam’s genotype consists of two homologous chromosomes, indexed by $j\in\{1,2\}$. The linkage phase at the interval between markers $k$ and $k+1$ describes the arrangement of alleles on these homologs. For an offspring $i$, the HMM’s hidden state at locus $k$, denoted $S_{ik}$, is the index of the maternal homolog transmitted to that offspring:
\[
S_{ik}\in\{1,2\}.
\]
If $S_{ik}=j$, the dam passed the segment of its $j$-th homolog containing marker $M_k$ to offspring $i$. Equivalently, write $A_k^{(j)}\in\{A,a\}$ for the allele carried on the $j$-th homolog at marker $k$. The observed data for offspring $i$ is a vector of genotype calls $(y_{i1},\ldots,y_{iz})$, where each $y_{ik}$ is coded as $\{2,1,0\}$ for genotypes $\{\mathrm{AA},\mathrm{Aa},\mathrm{aa}\}$ or $\varnothing$ for missing data. The dam’s genotype at locus $k$ is similarly denoted $G_k\in\{2,1,0\}$.

\subsection{Transition Model}\label{sec:transition}
The transition between hidden states $S_{ik}$ and $S_{i,k+1}$ depends on the recombination
fraction $r_k$ between adjacent markers $M_k$ and $M_{k+1}$. Since $S_{ik}\in\{1,2\}$ indexes
which maternal homolog is transmitted at marker $k$, a nonrecombinant meiosis preserves the
homolog index while a recombinant meiosis switches it. Thus, the transition probability matrix
$\mathbf T_k(j',j)=\Pr(S_{i,k+1}=j'\mid S_{ik}=j)$ is
\[
\mathbf T_k=
\begin{pmatrix}
1-r_k & r_k\\
r_k & 1-r_k
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
Linkage phase enters through the allele labels $A_k^{(j)}$ (or $A_k^{(d,j)}$ in the multi-dam setting) used in the emission model (Section~\ref{emission}).

\subsection{Joint Analysis Across Multiple Dams}
For $D$ dams, index families by $d\in\{1,\ldots,D\}$. We estimate a single recombination map
$\mathbf r=(r_1,\ldots,r_{z-1})$ shared across all dams, while allowing dam-specific linkage
phase configurations. Specifically, each dam $d$ has a phase configuration
$\boldsymbol{\psi}^{(d)}=(\psi_1^{(d)},\ldots,\psi_{z-1}^{(d)})$, where $\psi_k^{(d)}$
records whether the two alleles at markers $(k,k{+}1)$ are in coupling or repulsion.
For offspring $i$ of dam $d$, the hidden state $S_{ik}^{(d)}\in\{1,2\}$ indicates which
maternal homolog was transmitted at marker $k$. In the joint model the recombination map
$\mathbf r=(r_1,\ldots,r_{z-1})$ is \emph{shared} across dams, whereas the linkage phase
$\boldsymbol{\psi}^{(d)}$, the paternal gametic frequencies $q_k^{(d)}$, and hence the emissions
$b_k^{(i)}(j)$ are all \emph{dam-specific}. Consequently, in the EM the recombination counts are
pooled across dams (they inform the shared $\mathbf r$), while the paternal gamete counts that
update each $q_k^{(d)}$ use only that dam's offspring (Section~\ref{sec:par_cont}).

Given the dam's genotypes $\{G_k^{(d)}\}$, the phase array $\boldsymbol{\psi}^{(d)}$
induces a consistent labeling of alleles on the two maternal homologs along the marker order.
Concretely, after choosing the labeling at one anchor marker, each adjacent phase
$\psi_k^{(d)}$ specifies whether the homolog labels are carried forward unchanged
(coupling) or swapped (repulsion), yielding $\{A_k^{(d,1)},A_k^{(d,2)}\}_{k=1}^z$. For example, for markers $A$, $B$, and $C$, with coupling between $A-B$
and repulsion between $B-C$, the induced homolog labeling can be
represented by the ordered pairs
\[
(A_1^{(d,1)},A_1^{(d,2)})=(A,a),\quad
(A_2^{(d,1)},A_2^{(d,2)})=(B,b),\quad
(A_3^{(d,1)},A_3^{(d,2)})=(c,C).
\]
for $k=1,2,3$, respectively.

\subsection{Emission Model with an Unknown Paternal Contribution}
\label{emission}
The emission distribution links the observed offspring genotype $y_{ik}$ to the hidden maternal
state. We define
\[
b_k^{(i)}(j)=\Pr\!\big(y_{ik}\mid S_{ik}^{(d)}=j\big),
\]
the probability of observing $y_{ik}$ given that offspring $i$ inherited maternal homolog $j$
at marker $k$. Conditional on $S_{ik}^{(d)}=j$, the maternally transmitted allele at locus $k$
is the allele carried on that homolog, $A_k^{(d,j)}\in\{A,a\}$.

Throughout this single-dam derivation we suppress the dam superscript $d$ on $q_k^{(d)}$ (and on the
emission and posteriors) for readability; the multi-dam case restores it (Section~\ref{sec:par_cont}).
Because the transmitted paternal allele is unobserved, we marginalize the paternal contribution at
each marker. Let $\boldsymbol\pi_k=(\pi_{2,k},\pi_{1,k},\pi_{0,k})$ be a working paternal genotype
distribution at marker $k$ for $\{\mathrm{AA},\mathrm{Aa},\mathrm{aa}\}$ (nonnegative entries summing
to one) used only to carry out this marginalization; as shown below, the emission---and hence
everything the data identify---depends on $\boldsymbol\pi_k$ only through the single paternal gametic
frequency $q_k$, which is itself a parameter estimated in the EM (Section~\ref{sec:par_cont}). The
emission probability becomes the paternal-averaged Mendelian probability:
\begin{equation}
b_k^{(i)}(j)=\sum_{g\in\{2,1,0\}} \pi_{g,k}\,\theta_{ik}(j,g),
\qquad
\theta_{ik}(j,g):=\Pr\!\big(Y_{ik}=y_{ik}\mid A_k^{(d,j)},g\big),
\label{eq:emis-average}
\end{equation}
with the convention $b_k^{(i)}(j)=1$ if $y_{ik}=\varnothing$. The closed form of
$\theta_{ik}(j,g)$ follows by defining the A-allele indicator
\[
m_k^{(d,j)}=
\begin{cases}
1,& A_k^{(d,j)}=A,\\
0,& A_k^{(d,j)}=a,
\end{cases}
\]
and the paternal $A$-gamete probability
\[
p_g=
\begin{cases}
1,& g=2\ (\mathrm{AA}),\\[2pt]
\tfrac12,& g=1\ (\mathrm{Aa}),\\[2pt]
0,& g=0\ (\mathrm{aa}).
\end{cases}
\]
Then
\begin{equation}
\theta_{ik}(j,g)=
\begin{cases}
p_g,      & y_{ik}-m_k^{(d,j)}=1,\\[4pt]
1-p_g,    & y_{ik}-m_k^{(d,j)}=0,\\[4pt]
0,        & \text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:theta-cases}
\end{equation}
For example, if the dam transmits $A$ and the contributing paternal genotype is $\mathrm{Aa}$ then
$\Pr(\mathrm{AA})=\tfrac12$, $\Pr(\mathrm{Aa})=\tfrac12$, and $\Pr(\mathrm{aa})=0$.
If the dam is homozygous at marker $k$, then $A_k^{(d,1)}=A_k^{(d,2)}$ and hence
$b_k^{(i)}(1)=b_k^{(i)}(2)$, so such loci are uninformative about the transmitted homolog.

\paragraph{The identifiable paternal quantity is the gametic frequency.}
The paternal-averaged emission \eqref{eq:emis-average} depends on the paternal genotype vector
$\boldsymbol\pi_k$ only through the \emph{paternal gametic frequency}
\[
q_k \;=\; \sum_{g}\pi_{g,k}\,p_g
\;=\; \pi_{2,k}+\tfrac12\pi_{1,k}
\;=\; \pi_{\mathrm{AA},k}+\tfrac12\pi_{\mathrm{Aa},k}
\;=\; \Pr(\text{the paternal gamete transmits }A),
\]
with $p_g$ the paternal $A$-gamete probability defined above. Equivalently, before genotyping error
the emission is, over offspring genotypes $(\mathrm{AA},\mathrm{Aa},\mathrm{aa})$,
\[
b_k^{(i)}(j)=
\begin{cases}
(\,q_k,\;1-q_k,\;0\,) & \text{if } A_k^{(d,j)}=A,\\[2pt]
(\,0,\;q_k,\;1-q_k\,) & \text{if } A_k^{(d,j)}=a.
\end{cases}
\]
Consequently the offspring likelihood identifies \emph{only} $q_k$, not the three paternal genotype
frequencies $(\pi_{2,k},\pi_{1,k},\pi_{0,k})$ separately: any two genotype vectors with the same
$q_k=\pi_{\mathrm{AA},k}+\tfrac12\pi_{\mathrm{Aa},k}$ produce identical emissions and identical
likelihood. We therefore take $q_k$ (dam-specific $q_k^{(d)}$; superscript suppressed here) as the
single paternal parameter at each marker and dam, and treat $\boldsymbol\pi_k$ only as a redundant
working representation; we do not report or interpret paternal genotype frequencies or paternal
genotype distributions. Under assumption ($vii$), the paternal gamete allele is marginalized
independently across markers conditional on $q_k$.

\paragraph{Optional genotyping error:}
With a per-marker miscall rate $e_k\in[0,1)$ and symmetric errors among the three genotypes, use
\[
Q(y\mid y')=
\begin{cases}
1-e_k,& y=y',\\[4pt]
\dfrac{e_k}{2},& y\neq y',
\end{cases}
\qquad y,y'\in\{2,1,0\}.
\]
Then
\begin{equation}
b_k^{(i)}(j)
= \sum_{g\in\{2,1,0\}} \pi_{g,k}
   \Big[(1-e_k)\,\theta_{ik}(j,g)
        + \tfrac{e_k}{2}\,\big(1-\theta_{ik}(j,g)\big)\Big].
\label{eq:emission-error}
\end{equation}
A global $e$ may be used in place of a marker-specific $e_k$.

\subsubsection{Estimating the paternal gametic frequency $q_k$ in practice}
Because the emission depends on the paternal contribution only through $q_k$ (previous paragraph),
we estimate a single per-marker, dam-specific gametic frequency $q_k^{(d)}$ (superscript suppressed
in single-dam expressions), updated within the same Expectation--Maximization (EM) loop
\citep{Dempster1977} that estimates the shared map $\mathbf r$; full E- and M-step formulas appear
in Section~\ref{sec:par_cont}. The historical Hardy--Weinberg
parameterization $\boldsymbol{\pi}_k=[\,p_k^2,\,2p_k(1-p_k),\,(1-p_k)^2\,]$ is
\emph{mathematically identical} to the gametic model, because under that form
$q_k=p_k^2+\tfrac12\bigl(2p_k(1-p_k)\bigr)=p_k$; it is only a reparameterization of the same
one-parameter emission and is \emph{not} a separately identifiable biological model.

We regularize $q_k$ with a per-marker pseudocount prior $(\alpha_k,\beta_k)$. The M-step is the
penalized (maximum-a-posteriori) update
\[
\hat q_k=\frac{N_{A,k}+\alpha_k}{N_{A,k}+N_{a,k}+\alpha_k+\beta_k},
\]
where $N_{A,k}$ and $N_{a,k}$ are the \emph{direct} expected transmitted-paternal-gamete counts at
marker $k$---accumulated as posterior gamete responsibilities, not obtained from latent diploid
paternal-genotype counts (Section~\ref{sec:par_cont}); it maximizes
$\log\mathcal{L}+\sum_k\bigl[\alpha_k\log q_k+\beta_k\log(1-q_k)\bigr]$. Here $\alpha_k$ and
$\beta_k$ are pseudocounts, so the equivalent probability prior is
$\mathrm{Beta}(\alpha_k+1,\beta_k+1)$ and the update is its posterior mode;
$\alpha_k=\beta_k=0$ gives the unregularized fit. Writing the total pseudocount
$\lambda=\alpha_k+\beta_k$, the shrinkage target is the pseudocount proportion
$q^{(0)}_k=\alpha_k/(\alpha_k+\beta_k)$---equivalently the \emph{mode} of the
$\mathrm{Beta}(\alpha_k+1,\beta_k+1)$ prior, not its mean $(\alpha_k+1)/(\alpha_k+\beta_k+2)$---so
that $\alpha_k=\lambda q^{(0)}_k$ and $\beta_k=\lambda(1-q^{(0)}_k)$. The software default
$\lambda=20$ (i.e.\ $\alpha_k=\beta_k=10$, pseudocount target $q^{(0)}_k=0.5$) is retained for
backward compatibility only and is \emph{not} a statistically validated recommendation; it can
over-shrink $q_k$ at extreme paternal gametic frequencies.

\section{Likelihood Calculation and Parameter Estimation}
\subsection{Parameter Estimation Strategy}
\label{sec:param_estimation}
\paragraph{Overview}
We introduce an HMM for estimating recombination fractions \(\mathbf{r}\) along an ordered set of \(z\) markers, conditional on a known maternal linkage phase across the sequence. In principle one could search over all phase configurations and select the one that maximizes the multilocus likelihood. For \(z\) markers this requires evaluating \(2^{\,z-1}\) configurations, which is computationally infeasible even for modest \(z\).

To avoid exhaustive search, we use a two-step strategy. First, we perform a pairwise analysis that identifies, for each dam and for every selected marker pair (all pairs among the selected markers, not only adjacent ones), the most likely phase state (coupling or repulsion). We present the HMM conditional on a fixed phase for a sequence of \(z\) markers, then specialize to the case \(z=2\) to obtain a two-marker formulation. This specialization yields a simplification in which the two-marker likelihood derived from the HMM can be written as a multinomial model on genotype-class counts, leading to fast estimation and a natural LOD score for coupling versus repulsion (see Supplementary Section~\ref{sec:tpt}).

This step produces, for each dam, a complete matrix of pairwise phase calls and corresponding LOD scores. These are then supplied to the algorithm presented in Section~\ref{sec:global_phase_assembly} procedure, which resolves local conflicts and returns a single globally consistent phase configuration for each dam. The output is a fixed phase array.
\[
\bigl(\psi^{(d)}_1,\ldots,\psi^{(d)}_{z-1}\bigr),
\]
where \(\psi^{(d)}_k \in \{\mathrm{C},\mathrm{R}\}\) specifies the phase of the \(k\)-th adjacent interval for dam \(d\).

With this plan in place, we now present the HMM under a fixed linkage phase and define its likelihood, followed by the two-marker specialization used for pairwise phase calling.


\subsection{The Forward-Backward Algorithm}
The full forward variable for offspring $i$ at marker $k$ is denoted $\alpha_k^{(i)}(j)$. 
For readability, we suppress the offspring index $(i)$ and describe the algorithm for a single individual. In what follows, $\alpha_k(j)$ and $\beta_k(j)$ denote the scaled forward and backward variables (after applying the per-position factors $c_k$).


\subsubsection{The Forward Pass (\texorpdfstring{$\alpha$}{alpha})}
The forward variable, $\alpha_k(j)$, is the joint probability of observing the partial sequence of offspring genotypes up to marker $k$ \textit{and} being in hidden state $j$ at that marker \citep{Rabiner1989}. It accumulates evidence from the start of the sequence:
\begin{equation}
    \alpha_k(j) = \Pr(y_{i1},\ldots,y_{ik},\, S_{ik}=j).
\end{equation}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Initialization} ($k=1$): At the first marker, we assume an uninformative prior where each homolog has an equal chance of being transmitted ($1/2$):
\begin{equation}
    \alpha_1(j) = \frac{1}{2} b_1^{(i)}(j).
\end{equation}
Compute $c_1=\sum_{j=1}^2 \alpha_1(j)$ and rescale $\alpha_1(j)\leftarrow \alpha_1(j)/c_1$.
\item \textbf{Recursion} ($k=2, \dots, z$): To compute $\alpha_k(j')$, we sum over all possible previous states $j$ at $k-1$, weight by the transition probability, and multiply by the emission probability at $k$:
\begin{equation}
    \alpha_k(j') = \left[ \sum_{j=1}^{2} \alpha_{k-1}(j) \, \mathbf{T}_{k-1}(j',j) \right] b_k^{(i)}(j').
\end{equation}
\noindent where \(\mathbf{T}_{k-1}(j',j)\) denotes the transition probability for interval
\((k{-}1,k)\) as defined in Section~\ref{sec:transition}. After computing $\alpha_k(j')$, set $c_k=\sum_{j'=1}^2 \alpha_k(j')$ and rescale
$\alpha_k(j')\leftarrow \alpha_k(j')/c_k$.
Linkage phase is held fixed and enters through the emission probabilities \(b_k^{(i)}(j)\) via
the allele labels \(A_k^{(d,j)}\) (Section~\ref{emission}).
Rather than enumerating all \(2^{z-1}\) phase configurations (see Overview), we fix phase via the pairwise and global steps (Sections~\ref{sec:tpt}, \ref{sec:global_phase_assembly}). 
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{The Backward Pass (\texorpdfstring{$\beta$}{beta})}
The backward variable, $\beta_k(j)$, is the conditional probability of observing the sequence of offspring genotypes from marker $k+1$ to the end, \textit{given} that the hidden state at marker $k$ is $j$. It propagates evidence from the end of the sequence:
\begin{equation}
    \beta_k(j) = \Pr(y_{i,k+1},\ldots,y_{iz} \mid S_{ik}=j).
\end{equation}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Initialization ($k=z$): At the last marker, the probability of the (empty) future observation sequence is 1:
\begin{equation}
    \beta_z(j) = 1.
\end{equation}

\item Recursion ($k=z-1, \dots, 1$):  To compute $\beta_k(j)$, we sum over all possible next states $j'$ at $k+1$, weighting by the transition, emission, and future probability:
\begin{equation}
    \beta_k(j)=\frac{1}{c_{k+1}}\sum_{j'=1}^{2} \mathbf{T}_k(j',j)\, b_{k+1}^{(i)}(j')\, \beta_{k+1}(j').
\end{equation}
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Likelihood}
The likelihood for offspring $i$ is then $\Pr(O^{(i)}) = \prod_{k=1}^z c_k$, and the log-likelihood can be computed as
\begin{equation}
    \log \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{r}, \boldsymbol{\psi} \mid O^{(i)}) = \sum_{k=1}^z \log(c_k).
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Posterior Probabilities}
The forward and backward variables are combined to compute posterior probabilities, used for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm.

\paragraph{Single-Locus Posterior (\texorpdfstring{$\gamma$}{gamma}):} The probability of being in state $j$ at marker $k$, given all the observed data, is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:gamma}
    \gamma_k(j) = \Pr(S_{ik}=j \mid O^{(i)}) = \frac{\alpha_k(j)\beta_k(j)}{\sum_{\ell=1}^2 \alpha_k(\ell)\beta_k(\ell)}.
\end{equation}

\noindent where the denominator $\Pr(O^{(i)})$ is the likelihood of the data for offspring $i$, as calculated from the forward pass. Biologically, $\gamma_k(j)$ corresponds to the \textbf{haplotypic probability}, i.e., the probability of the offspring inheriting maternal haplotype $j$ at that locus given the multilocus model.

\paragraph{Adjacent-Locus Posterior (\texorpdfstring{$\xi$}{xi}):} The joint posterior of transitioning from state $j$ at marker $k$ to state $j'$ at marker $k+1$ is
\begin{equation}
    \xi_k(j,j') = \Pr(S_{ik}=j, S_{i,k+1}=j' \mid O^{(i)}) =
\frac{\alpha_k(j)\,\mathbf T_k(j',j)\,b_{k+1}^{(i)}(j')\,\beta_{k+1}(j')}{c_{k+1}}.
\end{equation}
Summing $\xi_k(j,j')$ over all individuals provides the expected counts of recombinant and non-recombinant events across interval $(k, k+1)$, which are then used to update the recombination fraction $r_k$ during the maximization step of the EM algorithm.

\subsection{EM Algorithm for Recombination Estimation}

With the linkage phases $(\boldsymbol{\psi}^{(1)}, \dots, \boldsymbol{\psi}^{(D)})$ for each dam held fixed, we estimate the shared recombination fractions $\mathbf{r}$ using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. This iterative approach, known as the Baum-Welch algorithm in the context of HMMs, is well suited for problems with missing data. Here, the "missing data" are the true counts of recombination and non-recombination events for each offspring at each marker interval.

\paragraph{The E-Step (Expectation):}
The E-step uses the posterior probabilities $\xi_k^{(i)}(j,j')$ from the forward-backward algorithm to compute the expected counts of recombination ($N_{\text{rec},k}$) and non-recombination ($N_{\text{nonrec},k}$) events for each interval $k$. Biologically, these counts correspond to the expected number of crossover events within that interval across the entire sample. 
These counts, summed over all $n$ offspring, depend on the posterior transition probabilities
$\xi_k^{(i)}(j,j')$. Under our state definition ($S_{ik}$ is the transmitted maternal homolog),
a recombination event in interval $(k,k{+}1)$ corresponds to a switch of homolog index
($j\neq j'$), regardless of the dam's linkage phase labeling.

\[
    N_{\text{rec},k} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left( \xi_k^{(i)}(1,2) + \xi_k^{(i)}(2,1) \right),
    \qquad
    N_{\text{nonrec},k} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left( \xi_k^{(i)}(1,1) + \xi_k^{(i)}(2,2) \right).
\]

and 

\[
N_{\text{total},k}
= \sum_{i=1}^{n}\sum_{j=1}^{2}\sum_{j'=1}^{2} \xi_k^{(i)}(j,j')
= N_{\text{rec},k}+N_{\text{nonrec},k}.
\]

\paragraph{The M-Step (Maximization):}
The M-step updates the recombination fractions by treating these expected counts as if they were observed data. The new estimate, $\hat{r}_k^{(t+1)}$, that maximizes the expected log-likelihood is the simple ratio:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{r}_k^{(t+1)} = \frac{N_{\text{rec},k}}{N_{\text{total},k}} = \frac{\text{Expected recombinations}}{\text{Total expected meioses}}.
\end{equation}
The estimation range is $r_k\in[\varepsilon,0.5]$ for a small $\varepsilon>0$: the M-step ratio is bounded above by the no-linkage value $0.5$ (enforced only by a numerical cap just below $0.5$), which is a \emph{permitted} estimate denoting no linkage; $r_k$ is \emph{not} constrained to lie strictly below $0.5$. An interval estimated at (or numerically at) $0.5$ is reported as a \emph{gap} in the map output---its map distance is \texttt{NA} and it is excluded from any map length (Section~\ref{sec:blocks})---rather than as a large finite distance. The E- and M-steps are alternated until the \emph{active objective} and the identifiable parameters $\mathbf r$ and $q$ are stable (see the Convergence and Decoding paragraph under Implementation Details), or a maximum iteration count is reached. The final parameter vector $\hat{\mathbf{r}}$ is a \emph{conditional} maximum-likelihood estimate, given the fixed linkage phase and the paternal gametic frequencies. The final recombination fractions can be converted to map distances in centimorgans (cM) using a mapping function, such as Haldane's or Kosambi's.
\paragraph{Paternal updates within the EM loop.}
The emission probabilities in Eqs.~\eqref{eq:emis-average}–\eqref{eq:emission-error} depend on the
dam-specific paternal gametic frequencies $\{q_k^{(d)}\}$. Each EM iteration proceeds as follows,
using each dam's own phase and emissions: (i) build $b_k^{(i)}(j)$ from the current $q_k^{(d)}$;
(ii) run forward--backward within each family to obtain $\gamma$ and $\xi$; (iii) update the
\emph{shared} map $\mathbf r$ from recombination counts \emph{pooled across all dams}; and (iv)
update each $q_k^{(d)}$ from the $\gamma$-weighted expected paternal gamete counts of the offspring
of dam $d$ only---these paternal counts are \emph{not} pooled across dams---using the penalized
update of Section~\ref{sec:par_cont}. All updates share a single Baum--Welch loop; full formulas are
provided in Supplementary Section~\ref{sec:par_cont}.

\paragraph{Homogeneity of recombination across dams.}
We treat the recombination map as shared across dams by default, reflecting its biological interpretation as a property of the chromosome. Empirically, female maps can differ across dams. To guard against bias when some dams contribute many offspring, we provide an optional heterogeneity check based on per-dam scalings of Haldane distances. Let $m_k=-\tfrac12\log(1-2r_k)$ be the Haldane distance for interval $k$. The scaled alternative sets $m_k^{(d)}=\eta^{(d)} m_k$, which implies
\[
r_k^{(d)}=\tfrac12\{1-\exp(-2\eta^{(d)} m_k)\}.
\]
This biological scaling is bounded only by the no-linkage value $0.5$ (approached as
$\eta^{(d)} m_k\to\infty$) and is \emph{not} capped at the map-reporting gap threshold; for
$\eta^{(d)}>1$ a scaled $r_k^{(d)}$ may exceed that reporting threshold while remaining below $0.5$.
This is a \emph{conditional global-scale} likelihood-ratio test, and it is a global-scale contrast rather than a test of interval-specific heterogeneity: the interval \emph{shape} $m_k$ of the shared map is held fixed and only its overall length is rescaled per dam. The null allows a single \emph{common} global scale $\eta$ shared by all dams (one free parameter, estimated---not fixed to $1$), and the alternative a separate $\eta^{(d)}$ per dam ($D$ free parameters), giving a likelihood-ratio statistic on $D-1$ degrees of freedom. Each $\eta^{(d)}$ is profiled by a one-dimensional search with the shared interval shape, the per-dam linkage phase, and the per-dam paternal parameters $q_k^{(d)}$ \emph{held fixed}; the statistic is therefore conditional on the fitted phase and paternal nuisance parameters. The chi-square calibration is asymptotic; for small numbers of dams, small families, or a scale estimate at the search boundary, a parametric bootstrap should be used for a definitive $p$-value.


\subsection{Global Phase Assembly}
\label{sec:global_phase_assembly}

Rather than embedding phase estimation within the HMM, which would introduce an additional latent layer and substantially complicate the EM updates, we estimate the full chromosomal phase externally in a single step. We map pairwise phase calls to signs (\(+1\) for coupling, \(-1\) for repulsion) and weight them by the corresponding LOD scores to build a symmetric, signed, LOD-weighted adjacency matrix \(J\) over markers. We seek the assignment of markers to the two maternal homologs that maximizes a LOD-weighted agreement criterion, equivalently \(\max_{x\in\{-1,+1\}^T} \sum_{i<j} J_{ij} x_i x_j\). This combinatorial graph objective is \emph{approximately} optimized---by a spectral initialization followed by greedy coordinate ascent (below)---rather than solved exactly; the procedure is a heuristic and is not guaranteed to reach the global optimum.

The graph is built \emph{per dam} from that dam's own phase-LOD matrix (not a pooled LOD), using only supported edges (finite LOD above a support threshold). It is split into connected components; within a component we initialize \(x\) using the leading eigenvector of \(J\), following signed-graph spectral ideas \citep{Kunegis2010SpectralVisualization}, and refine with greedy coordinate ascent (a deterministic best-improvement rule). This is a \emph{heuristic} and is not guaranteed to reach the global optimum. The relative orientation of different components is unidentified: intervals crossing a component boundary are returned as \emph{unresolved} rather than forced to coupling, and a region with no supported edges is reported as unresolved rather than as a resolved phase. This yields a dam-specific phase configuration \((\boldsymbol{\psi}^{(1)},\dots,\boldsymbol{\psi}^{(D)})\) with explicit resolved/unresolved metadata. A concise algorithmic description appears in Supplementary Section~\ref{sec:graph_phase}.


\section{Implementation}\label{sec:implementation}

\paragraph{GitHub repository:} \url{https://github.com/mmollina/HSMap}

\paragraph{Initialization and Constraints:} We use an uninformative prior for the initial hidden states at the left boundary (uniform prior $\,\Pr(S_{i1}=1)=\Pr(S_{i1}=2)=\tfrac12$). Recombination fractions are initialized uniformly (by default $r_k=0.05$) and are estimated over the interval $[\varepsilon, 0.5]$ during optimization for a small $\varepsilon > 0$ (numerically capped just below $0.5$; $r_k=0.5$ is permitted and denotes no linkage, reported as a gap). Each dam-specific paternal gametic frequency $q_k^{(d)}$ is initialized at $0.5$; the default total pseudocount is $\lambda=20$ (i.e.\ $\alpha_k=\beta_k=10$, pseudocount target $q^{(0)}_k=0.5$), retained for backward compatibility only and not a statistically validated recommendation (Section~\ref{sec:par_cont}).

\paragraph{Convergence and Decoding:} The EM algorithm is run until (i) the relative change in the \emph{active objective}---the observed-data log-likelihood when $\lambda=0$, or the penalized objective (observed-data log-likelihood plus the $q$ pseudocount penalty) when $\lambda>0$---falls below a tolerance of $10^{-6}$, \emph{and} (ii) the maximum change in the identifiable parameters $\mathbf r$ and $q$ is below the same tolerance; or until a maximum number of iterations is reached (default $1000$; the wrapper warns on non-convergence). Convergence is \emph{not} gated on movement along the non-identifiable paternal genotype decomposition. The reported log-likelihood is evaluated at the final returned parameters (after the last M-step). The recombination fractions are conditional maximum-likelihood estimates in $[\varepsilon, 0.5]$ (with $r=0.5$ permitted when the data indicate no linkage), whereas each $q_k^{(d)}$ is a penalized/maximum-a-posteriori estimate when $\lambda>0$ and an unpenalized maximum-likelihood estimate when $\lambda=0$. The emission error rate is a single user-specified scalar $\varepsilon$; the model does not fit a per-dam or per-marker error rate. After convergence, the final model parameters can be used to decode the maternal transmissions for each offspring. The posterior probabilities ($\gamma_k(j)$) provide the probability of inheriting from each homolog at each locus (a most-probable-path / Viterbi decoder is not currently provided). These decoded paths are valuable for downstream analyses, such as identifying specific crossover locations, error checking or quantitative trait loci (QTL) analysis.

\paragraph{Computational Notes:} For $n$ offspring and $z$ markers, the forward-backward algorithm has a time complexity of $O(nz)$ and constant memory per individual. The computations are trivially parallelizable across offspring. To prevent numerical underflow, we use per-position scaling, where the individual log-likelihood is the sum of the logs of the scaling factors. Loci where the dam is homozygous are uninformative for phase but are retained in the model.

\paragraph{Equivalent allele-state parameterization.} The hidden state defined above indexes the transmitted maternal homolog, so the transition matrix is phase-free and linkage phase enters only through the emission allele labels $A_k^{(d,j)}$ (Section~\ref{sec:transition}). The implementation uses a mathematically equivalent reparameterization in which the hidden state is the transmitted \emph{allele} ($a$ or $A$). Emissions then depend only on the transmitted allele and are phase-free, while the fixed allele-to-homolog labeling implied by the phase makes linkage phase enter the \emph{transition}: a nonrecombinant interval preserves the allele under coupling, so $\Pr(\text{same allele})=1-r_k$, and switches it under repulsion, so $\Pr(\text{same allele})=r_k$. The two formulations yield identical per-individual likelihoods and recombination estimates; the allele-state form avoids relabeling emissions whenever the phase changes, and homolog posteriors are recovered by a cumulative exclusive-or over repulsion intervals. Equivalently, in the M-step a recombination in interval $k$ is the homolog-index switch of Section~\ref{sec:transition}, which the allele-state code scores as an allele switch under coupling and an allele match under repulsion.

\paragraph{Paternal linkage disequilibrium and pollen-pool heterogeneity (limitation).} The paternal contribution is marginalized one marker at a time: the paternal gamete alleles are treated as independent across markers (assumption $vii$), so paternal linkage disequilibrium (LD) across markers is not modeled, and the gametic $q^{(d)}$ model does not represent paternal haplotypes. When paternal LD is present, the offspring two-locus genotype distribution departs from the independent-gamete form assumed here, which can bias the paternal (pollen-pool) gametic-frequency estimates and, through them, confound maternal phase or recombination inference---most directly in the pairwise phase stage (Section~\ref{sec:tpt}), whose likelihood assumes independent paternal alleles at the two markers. Both the pairwise stage and the multipoint model estimate a separate paternal gametic frequency per dam ($p_k^{(d)}$ and $q_k^{(d)}$, respectively); neither, however, models cross-marker paternal LD. The method therefore does not claim to resolve unknown-paternity structure completely; accounting for paternal LD across markers is left to future work.

\section{Discussion}
\textsf{HSMap} takes the position that, in open-pollinated families, the unknown
paternal contribution should be modelled explicitly rather than discarded. Marginalizing
the paternal allele through a per-marker gametic frequency yields a proper likelihood for
maternal transmission and, importantly, keeps that nuisance parameter interpretable: it
is a pollen-pool transmission probability, estimated per dam because different dams may be
pollinated by different pollen compositions. Sharing a single recombination map across
dams while keeping phase and the paternal model dam-specific reflects the biology---the
female map is a property of the species/chromosome, whereas phase and pollen composition
are properties of the individual family---and it lets the joint EM pool recombination
information across families that are informative at different markers. The estimator pools
\emph{recombination counts} into the shared map; it does not pool phase or the paternal
frequencies, which remain dam-specific.

Two design choices deserve emphasis. First, phase is inferred outside the HMM and held
fixed. This avoids an extra latent layer and a much harder joint optimization, at the
cost of not propagating phase uncertainty; the workflow mitigates the risk by refusing to
impute phase where the pairwise evidence does not support it, returning explicit
unresolved intervals instead. Second, the multipoint map is fitted only within resolved
phase blocks, and unlinked intervals are reported as gaps. This prevents the common
artefact whereby an interval with a recombination fraction near one-half is converted into
a very large centimorgan distance, and it makes the boundary between ``mapped'' and ``not
mapped'' explicit. Relative to full-sib mapping with two known, phased parents, the
open-pollinated model recovers less information per family---paternal transmission is not
tracked---but it applies where full-sib designs are unavailable, and pooling across dams
recovers precision. Extending the framework to \emph{known}-sire full-sib crosses, in
which paternal transmission is itself a linked hidden path, is a natural next step and is
under development (Section~\ref{sec:limitations}).

\section{Limitations}\label{sec:limitations}
Several limitations bound the current method. (i) \emph{Paternal linkage disequilibrium.}
The paternal allele is marginalized independently at each marker, so cross-marker paternal
LD is not modelled; when present it can bias the pollen-pool frequency estimates and,
through the pairwise phasing stage, maternal inference. (ii) \emph{Fixed phase.} Phase is
a hard external call held fixed during HMM fitting, so phase uncertainty is not propagated
into the map; intervals where phase cannot be resolved are reported as gaps rather than
guessed. (iii) \emph{Heterogeneity test.} The optional test for a shared map across dams
is a \emph{conditional, global-scale} likelihood-ratio test---it rescales the overall map
length per dam with the interval shape held fixed, conditional on the fitted phase and
paternal nuisance parameters---and its $\chi^2$ calibration is asymptotic; it is not a test
of interval-specific heterogeneity. (iv) \emph{Unvalidated defaults.} The paternal
pseudocount ($\lambda$), the phase-support threshold, and the no-linkage threshold
(\texttt{gap\_r}) are retained as compatibility values and have not yet been optimized by
simulation; they are labelled as such in the software. (v) \emph{Completeness.} Map
completeness depends on dam informativeness: a chromosome that no dam resolves cannot be
mapped across the unresolved region. (vi) \emph{Scope.} The present method covers
open-pollinated / unknown-sire families; known-sire full-sib support is experimental and
not part of this work.

\section{Conclusions}
\textsf{HSMap} provides an explicit, reproducible framework for building maternal linkage
maps in open-pollinated diploid half-sib families. By integrating out the unknown paternal
contribution through a dam-specific gametic frequency, pooling recombination information
across dams in a joint EM, inferring phase with honest unresolved-interval reporting, and
fitting maps blockwise with gap-aware summaries, it turns an otherwise awkward design into
a statistically principled workflow.

\section{Supplementary Material} \label{sec:sup_mat}
\subsection{Paternal gametic frequency: E-step and M-step} \phantomsection \label{sec:par_cont}
The paternal contribution is summarized, for each dam \(d\) and marker \(k\), by a single gametic
frequency \(q_k^{(d)}=\Pr(\text{a paternal gamete of an offspring of dam }d\text{ transmits }A)\),
with the paternal gamete allele marginalized independently across markers (assumption $vii$). Its
update is embedded in the same Baum--Welch loop used to estimate the \emph{shared} map
\(\mathbf r\). For an offspring \(i\) of dam \(d\), let
\[
\begin{aligned}
e^{A}_{ik}(j)&=\Pr\!\big(Y_{ik}=y_{ik}\mid A_k^{(d,j)},\ \text{paternal gamete }A\big),\\
e^{a}_{ik}(j)&=\Pr\!\big(Y_{ik}=y_{ik}\mid A_k^{(d,j)},\ \text{paternal gamete }a\big)
\end{aligned}
\]
be the error-convolved single-locus probabilities of the observed genotype given the transmitted
maternal allele \(A_k^{(d,j)}\) and the paternal gamete allele (Eq.~\eqref{eq:emission-error}), so
the emission is \(b_k^{(i)}(j)=q_k^{(d)}\,e^{A}_{ik}(j)+(1-q_k^{(d)})\,e^{a}_{ik}(j)\). Each outer
iteration, run separately within each family using that dam's phase, (i) builds \(b_k^{(i)}(j)\)
from the current \(q_k^{(d)}\); (ii) runs forward--backward to obtain the per-family posteriors
\(\gamma_k^{(i,d)}(j)\) and \(\xi_k^{(i,d)}(j,j')\); (iii) updates the shared \(\mathbf r\) from
recombination counts pooled across all dams (Section~\ref{sec:param_estimation}); and (iv) updates
each \(q_k^{(d)}\) from that dam's offspring only, as follows.

\paragraph{E-step: expected paternal gamete counts (per dam).}
For an offspring \(i\) of dam \(d\) at marker \(k\), the posterior probability that its paternal
gamete transmitted \(A\), marginalized over the maternal-state posterior \(\gamma_k^{(i,d)}(j)\)
with the emission normalized \emph{per maternal state}, is
\[
\rho_{A,k}^{(i,d)}=\sum_{j\in\{1,2\}}\gamma_k^{(i,d)}(j)\,
\frac{q_k^{(d)}\,e^{A}_{ik}(j)}{b_k^{(i)}(j)},
\qquad
\rho_{a,k}^{(i,d)}=\sum_{j\in\{1,2\}}\gamma_k^{(i,d)}(j)\,
\frac{(1-q_k^{(d)})\,e^{a}_{ik}(j)}{b_k^{(i)}(j)},
\]
which sum to \(1\) at observed loci. A maternal state \(j\) contributes only when both
\(\gamma_k^{(i,d)}(j)>0\) and \(b_k^{(i)}(j)>0\), so an impossible state at zero genotyping error is
skipped rather than dividing by zero. Missing calls (\(y_{ik}=\varnothing\)) contribute no gamete
count. Summing these \emph{direct} transmitted-gamete responsibilities over the offspring
\emph{of dam \(d\)} gives the dam-specific expected transmitted-paternal-gamete counts
\[
N_{A,k}^{(d)}=\sum_{i\,\in\,\text{family }d}\rho_{A,k}^{(i,d)},\qquad
N_{a,k}^{(d)}=\sum_{i\,\in\,\text{family }d}\rho_{a,k}^{(i,d)}.
\]
These are transmitted-gamete counts accumulated \emph{directly} from the per-maternal-state
normalized responsibilities above; they are not derived from latent diploid paternal-genotype
counts (which, carrying two alleles per genotype, would give the pseudocount prior about twice its
nominal weight). The paternal gamete counts are \emph{not} pooled across dams---unlike the
recombination counts, which are pooled because \(\mathbf r\) is shared.

\paragraph{M-step: penalized (MAP) update (per dam).}
With a per-marker, per-dam pseudocount prior \((\alpha_k^{(d)},\beta_k^{(d)})\),
\[
\hat q_k^{(d)}=\frac{N_{A,k}^{(d)}+\alpha_k^{(d)}}
{N_{A,k}^{(d)}+N_{a,k}^{(d)}+\alpha_k^{(d)}+\beta_k^{(d)}}.
\]
This maximizes the penalized objective
\(\log\mathcal{L}+\sum_{d,k}\bigl[\alpha_k^{(d)}\log q_k^{(d)}+\beta_k^{(d)}\log(1-q_k^{(d)})\bigr]\);
equivalently \(\hat q_k^{(d)}\) is the posterior mode under a
\(\mathrm{Beta}(\alpha_k^{(d)}+1,\beta_k^{(d)}+1)\) prior, and \(\alpha_k^{(d)}=\beta_k^{(d)}=0\)
gives the unregularized fit. Writing the total pseudocount \(\lambda=\alpha_k^{(d)}+\beta_k^{(d)}\),
the shrinkage target is the pseudocount proportion (the prior \emph{mode})
\(q^{(0)}_k=\alpha_k^{(d)}/(\alpha_k^{(d)}+\beta_k^{(d)})\), so \(\alpha_k^{(d)}=\lambda q^{(0)}_k\)
and \(\beta_k^{(d)}=\lambda(1-q^{(0)}_k)\); the \emph{mean} of the equivalent Beta prior is instead
\((\alpha_k^{(d)}+1)/(\alpha_k^{(d)}+\beta_k^{(d)}+2)\). The software default \(\lambda=20\)
(\(\alpha_k^{(d)}=\beta_k^{(d)}=10\), pseudocount target \(q^{(0)}_k=0.5\)) is retained for backward
compatibility only and is not a statistically validated recommendation.

\paragraph{Practical notes.}
(i) Initialization: \(q^{(0)}_k=0.5\) when no prior is supplied.
(ii) Missing data: if all calls at marker \(k\) are missing for dam \(d\), \(\hat q_k^{(d)}\) reverts
to the pseudocount target \(q^{(0)}_k\).
(iii) Numerical stability: clip \(\hat q_k^{(d)}\) to \([10^{-9},\,1-10^{-9}]\).
(iv) The Hardy--Weinberg genotype form \(\boldsymbol{\pi}_k=[\,p_k^2,\,2p_k(1-p_k),\,(1-p_k)^2\,]\)
with \(p_k=q_k^{(d)}\) yields identical emissions and is only a reparameterization of this
one-parameter model, not a separately identifiable model.

\subsection{Two-Point Analysis for Pairwise Phase and Recombination Estimation}
\label{sec:tpt}
To initialize the multilocus HMM and to build the phasing graph, we fit a two-marker model for \emph{every selected marker pair} $(k,k')$ (all pairs among the selected markers, not only adjacent ones) that compares the two maternal phase hypotheses and estimates a pairwise recombination fraction. The pairwise model currently assumes \emph{zero genotyping error} (error is handled only in the multipoint HMM). We consider only dams that are double heterozygotes at these markers \(\bigl(G_k^{(d)}=G_{k'}^{(d)}=1\bigr)\). For each such dam $d$, we count the number of offspring, $C_d(a,b)$, for each two-locus genotype $(y_{ik},y_{i,k'})=(a,b)\in\{0,1,2\}^2$. Offspring with a missing call at exactly one locus are \emph{not discarded}: their contribution is the correct single-marker marginal (summing the two-locus probability over the missing genotype); an offspring missing at both loci contributes nothing. Partial (single-marker) observations enter both the reported pairwise likelihood and the estimate of the paternal frequency at the observed marker.

\paragraph{Two-marker offspring genotype distribution under phase and $r_{kk'}$.}
The probability of an offspring's two-locus genotype, $(Y_k,Y_{k'})=(y_k,y_{k'})$, depends on the maternal phase, the recombination fraction $r_{kk'}$, and \emph{dam-specific} paternal gametic frequencies $(p_k^{(d)},p_{k'}^{(d)})$. We build this probability in three steps (the dam superscript is suppressed below).

\subsubsection{Step 1: Paternal contribution at a single locus}
Let $p_k^{(d)}$ be the frequency of allele $A$ among the paternal gametes of dam $d$ at marker $k$.
It is estimated \emph{separately for each dam} (not pooled across dams), by the same identifiable
gametic quantity used in the multipoint model: under the zero-error pairwise model an offspring
genotype $AA$ observes one paternal $A$ transmission and $aa$ one paternal $a$ transmission (a
heterozygous offspring is uninformative), so with a per-marker pseudocount prior
$(\alpha_k,\beta_k)$,
\[
\hat p_k^{(d)} = \frac{n^{(d)}_{AA,k}+\alpha_k}{n^{(d)}_{AA,k}+n^{(d)}_{aa,k}+\alpha_k+\beta_k},
\]
counted over \emph{all} offspring of dam $d$ with an observed call at marker $k$ (including those
missing at the paired marker). Define the row vectors, listed in the order $(Y=2,\,Y=1,\,Y=0)$,
\[
F_k^{(A)} := (\,p_k,\,1-p_k,\,0\,), \qquad
F_k^{(a)} := (\,0,\,p_k,\,1-p_k\,).
\]
Let $F_k^{(m)}(y)$ denote the component of $F_k^{(m)}$ corresponding to genotype $Y=y$,
under the ordering $(y=2,\,1,\,0)$.
At marker $k{+}1$ we use alleles $B/b$ and define analogously
\[
F_{k'}^{(B)} := (\,p_{k'},\,1-p_{k'},\,0\,), \qquad
F_{k'}^{(b)} := (\,0,\,p_{k'},\,1-p_{k'}\,).
\]

\subsubsection{Step 2: Maternal gamete frequencies}
For a doubly heterozygous dam, gamete frequencies depend on phase and $r_{kk'}$.
In \emph{coupling} (haplotypes $AB$ and $ab$):
\[
\text{nonrecombinants } AB,ab:\ \tfrac{1-r_{kk'}}{2}\ \text{each}, \qquad
\text{recombinants } Ab,aB:\ \tfrac{r_{kk'}}{2}\ \text{each}.
\]
In \emph{repulsion} (haplotypes $Ab$ and $aB$), the nonrecombinants are $Ab,aB$ and the recombinants are $AB,ab$ with the same weights.

\subsubsection{Step 3: Combined two-locus probability}
Summing over the four maternal gametes gives, for coupling,
\[
\begin{aligned}
P^{(C)}_{y_k,y_{k'}}(r_{kk'};p_k,p_{k'})
=\;&\frac{1-r_{kk'}}{2}\Big[\,F_k^{(A)}(y_k)\,F_{k'}^{(B)}(y_{k'})
      + F_k^{(a)}(y_k)\,F_{k'}^{(b)}(y_{k'})\Big] \\
&+\frac{r_{kk'}}{2}\Big[\,F_k^{(A)}(y_k)\,F_{k'}^{(b)}(y_{k'})
      + F_k^{(a)}(y_k)\,F_{k'}^{(B)}(y_{k'})\Big].
\end{aligned}
\]
The expression for repulsion is obtained by swapping the nonrecombinant and recombinant pairings. At $r_{kk'}=0.5$ the two phases are indistinguishable.

\paragraph{Estimation and phase calls (dam-specific $p^{(d)}$ and phase; shared $r_{kk'}$).}
The paternal frequencies $\hat p_k^{(d)}$ are estimated \emph{per dam} as above. The log-likelihood
for dam $d$ (complete pairs contributing the two-locus term and single-marker observations the
corresponding marginal) is
\[
\ell_d(r_{kk'},\phi) \;=\; \sum_{a=0}^{2}\sum_{b=0}^{2} C_d(a,b)\,\log P^{(\phi)}_{ab}(r_{kk'};\hat p_k^{(d)},\hat p_{k'}^{(d)}) \;+\; (\text{single-marker marginal terms}).
\]
Crucially, phase is decided \emph{within each maternal family}. We treat phase as a dam-specific nuisance parameter and profile it out while estimating a single recombination fraction shared across the informative dams:
\[
\hat r_{kk'} \;=\; \arg\max_{\,r\in[\varepsilon,\,0.5]}\ \sum_{d}\ \max_{\phi\in\{C,R\}}\ \ell_d(r,\phi),
\]
where the search interval \emph{includes $r=0.5$ exactly} so the no-linkage null is evaluated at
$r=0.5$ (never at $0.49$), and $r_{kk'}=0.5$ is a permitted estimate. Because this profiled
objective is a sum over dams of a maximum of two phase-specific likelihoods it need not be unimodal;
it is maximized by a coarse grid over $[\varepsilon,0.5]$ (including $0.5$) followed by bounded local
refinement of the promising neighborhoods, and the LOD versus no linkage is
$\mathrm{LOD}_r = [\ell(\hat r_{kk'})-\ell(0.5)]/\ln 10$. Phase LOD is returned \emph{separately by
dam}. Given $\hat r_{kk'}$, the final phase call for dam $d$ is
\[
\hat\phi_d \;=\; \arg\max_{\phi\in\{C,R\}} \ell_d(\hat r_{kk'},\phi),
\]
and its statistical support is summarized by a LOD score, for example,
\[
\mathrm{LOD}_d \;=\; \frac{\ell_d(\hat r_{kk'},\hat\phi_d)-\ell_d(\hat r_{kk'},\phi_{\mathrm{alt}})}{\ln 10}.
\]
This per-dam decision avoids any exponential search over phases across dams and keeps computation linear in the number of families.

\subsection{Graph-based maternal phasing from pairwise evidence} \label{sec:graph_phase}

\noindent\textbf{Inputs (dam-specific).}
The phasing graph is built \emph{separately for each dam} from that dam's own pairwise phase calls
and its own phase-LOD matrix (not a pooled LOD). For a given dam let \(A\in\{0,1,\text{NA}\}^{z\times z}\)
be the pairwise maternal phase matrix (\(1=\) coupling, \(0=\) repulsion, NA uninformative),
\(W^{(d)}\in\mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}^{z\times z}\) that dam's phase-LOD matrix, and
\(\mathcal{O}=(M_1,\ldots,M_z)\) the marker order.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Supported edges and graph.}
An edge \((i,j)\) is \emph{supported} only when its phase sign is available, its LOD is finite, and its
LOD strictly exceeds a support threshold \(\tau=\max(\texttt{min\_phase\_lod},\texttt{tie\_tol})\); edges
with zero or near-tie evidence are never treated as informative. On the supported edges, map \(A\) to
signs \(S\) (\(+1\) coupling, \(-1\) repulsion) and form the symmetric signed adjacency
\(J=\tfrac{1}{2}(S\odot W^{(d)}+(S\odot W^{(d)})^{\top})\).

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Connected components.}
The supported-edge graph is split into connected components. Within each component the marker
orientations are determined up to a single global sign, so the \emph{relative} phase of any two
markers in the same component is identified, whereas the relative orientation of \emph{different}
components is \textbf{unidentified}. A marker with no supported edges is its own singleton component.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Spectral + greedy heuristic (per component).}
Within each component we seed \(x_i=\operatorname{sign}(v_{1,i})\) from the leading eigenvector of the
component's \(J\), then apply greedy coordinate ascent that repeatedly flips the marker with the
largest positive improvement \(\Delta_i=-2\,x_i\,(Jx)_i\) until no flip improves the objective beyond
\texttt{tol} or \texttt{max\_passes} is reached (a deterministic best-improvement rule, not a random
marker order). This is a \emph{heuristic}: it is not guaranteed to find the global optimum of the
component's quadratic objective. Each component's first marker is sign-anchored; an optional global
anchor fixes the sign of one chosen component.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Output and unresolved phase.}
Report per-marker homolog labels (meaningful only within a component), the component index of each
marker, and, for each adjacent interval, the phase where it is resolved:
\[
  \text{phase}[k] \;=\; \mathbb{I}[c_k=c_{k+1}] \quad\text{if } M_k, M_{k+1} \text{ share a component},
  \qquad \text{else } \texttt{NA (unresolved)}.
\]
Intervals whose endpoints lie in different components are returned as \emph{unresolved}, not as
coupling; a graph with no supported edges yields all-singleton components and an explicit warning,
never a resolved all-coupling result. We also report the per-component objective, whether greedy
refinement converged, the number of components and their sizes, and the unresolved markers and
intervals. A resolved adjacent interval is resolved either \emph{directly}---through its own $(t,t{+}1)$
supported edge---or \emph{indirectly, through a path} within its connected component. Each interval
reports \texttt{direct\_lod}, the \emph{raw} finite LOD of the adjacent $(t,t{+}1)$ edge
(\texttt{NA} when that edge is absent or non-finite), which may be zero or below threshold for a
path-resolved interval; \texttt{direct\_supported}, whether that adjacent edge passes the phase,
finite-value, tie, and threshold rules; and \texttt{resolved\_via}, recording ``direct'', ``path'',
or ``unresolved''. The field \texttt{interval\_support} is retained as a strict alias of
\texttt{direct\_lod}. A raw \texttt{direct\_lod} that is zero or below threshold is therefore
\emph{not} read as the total support for a path-resolved interval. Because
phase is a hard call fixed downstream, phase uncertainty is not propagated; this is a limitation.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Multi-dam case.}
The construction is applied independently to each dam (each with its own \(W^{(d)}\)) and a named list
is returned; the pooled LOD is retained only as an overall summary.

\subsection{Blockwise multipoint fitting and map reporting} \label{sec:blocks}
The multipoint HMM (Section~\ref{sec:param_estimation}) requires a known relative phase for every
fitted interval, so it is fitted \emph{only within resolved phase blocks}. The marker order is split
wherever an adjacent interval is unresolved (an unidentified relative orientation between phase
components); phase is never imputed across such a boundary, and no recombination estimate is produced
across it. For a single dam the split is at each unresolved interval. For a joint multi-dam map, a
conservative common-boundary rule places a boundary at an interval when \emph{either} no dam
resolves that interval \emph{or} any dam that carries phase information there is unresolved. A dam
with no phase information at the interval does not by itself force a boundary when another dam
resolves it; but an interval that no dam resolves is always a boundary, so a single unsupported
interval never discards an otherwise resolved chromosome-wide block on either side of it. Each
block's joint fit uses only the dams whose phase is resolved throughout that block (the contributing
dams are reported), and for every boundary we report the reason together with the sets of dams that
are resolved, informative-but-unresolved, and carrying no phase information there. Phase is treated
as fixed within each fitted block.

For map reporting, an interval whose estimated $r$ is at or near $0.5$ (no linkage), or whose phase is
unresolved, is reported as a \emph{gap}: its map distance is \texttt{NA} and it is excluded from any
within-block map length, so an unlinked interval never becomes a large finite centimorgan distance.
Within a resolved phase block, a fitted no-linkage boundary (an interval estimated at no linkage)
starts a new \emph{map segment}: cumulative positions are reported within each segment and reset at
every gap, so one phase block may contain several segments and a position never carries across a gap.
Each marker's phase block, map segment, and within-segment position are reported. The reported map
length is the sum of the finite, linked interval distances within blocks; a per-interval status
(\texttt{linked}, \texttt{no\_linkage\_boundary}, \texttt{unresolved\_phase}, \texttt{between\_blocks},
\texttt{insufficient\_information}) accompanies the map.
